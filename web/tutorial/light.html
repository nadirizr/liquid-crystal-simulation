The wave nature of light

Light has, as you might know, a dualistic nature; it can be treated as a stream of particles (photons) or as a wave motion. When we deal with the optical effects of liquid crystals we sometimes use the first approach to treat light scattering, but normally the latter is used to treat the large number of interesting and often unique optical phenomena. The light wave is a transverse electromagnetic wave motion. This means that it consists of two coupled, electric and magnetic, fields oscillating in a plane perpendicular to the beam direction (see figure 3). To simplify things, however, we often use only the electric field vector (E-field) when describing light.

 
Figure 3. A light wave is a transverse electromagnetic wave, that is it is a combination of an oscillating electric field and an oscillating magnetic field. The fields oscillate perpendicular to each other and both fields oscillate in a plane perpendicular to the propagation direction.

If we study a fix point in space passed by a light beam and let time pass, the electric field vector will describe a more or less complex or chaotic motion in the oscillation plane. It may for instance oscillate along a line (figure 4a) or it may rotate in the plane (figure 4b). The manner in which the light oscillates is described as its state of polarization. The E-field vector of linearly polarized light describes a motion like in figure 4a (upper part), while figure 4b (upper part) describes circularly polarized light. Both are special cases of the general case, eliptically polarized light. In general the motion may be understood as a superposition of two perpendicular linearly polarized components (or two circularly polarized components with opposite handedness) with a well defined phase relationship, as described in the figure. Most light is, however, more or less unpolarized, which means that only to some degree there is a constant phase relation between the two components; in the completely unpolarized state there is a random motion of the electric field vector in the transverse plane.

 
Figure 4a. Linearly polarized light (red arrows) can be thought of as the superposition of one linearly polarized component oscillating vertically (green arrows) in phase with another, oscillating horizontally (blue arrows). The parameter t is time and T is the oscillation period.

 
Figure 4b. Circularly polarized light (red arrows) can be thought of as the superposition of one linearly polarized component oscillating vertically (green arrows) 90 degrees out of phase from another, oscillating horizontally (blue arrows). The parameter t is time and T is the oscillation period.
